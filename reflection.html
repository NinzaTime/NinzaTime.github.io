Course Reflection

The transition to the rhetoric of technical communication has been a process of translating objective logic into strategic influence. Earlier, I thought about writing as an instrument for transmitting information and documentation. This course has completely changed my approach, making me realize that successful writing goes beyond information transfer and involves the skillful handling of readers' expectations within different social situations.

Project 1 served as my first major deep dive into investigative research. Exploring Post-Quantum Cryptography required me to balance complex mathematical concepts with a narrative that a general academic audience could follow. I learned that data is only as good as the context provided for it, and that establishing a research exigence is the first step in technical credibility.

In the Project 1 Reflection, I analyzed my own struggle with the word count. Initially, I saw 2,000 words as an arbitrary goal. However, I realized that the "extra" space was actually necessary to build the logical bridges required for a non-expert to understand lattice-based math, transitioning my view from quantity to quality.

Project 2 pushed me to transition from investigation to high-stakes persuasion. Creating a campaign for three different audiences—CEOs, Engineers, and Students—taught me that "truth" is often less important than "relevance." If a CEO doesn't see the financial risk, the technical truth of a quantum threat is ignored by the decision-makers.

My Project 2 Reflection focused on the difficulty of switching "voices." It was a meta-cognitive exercise that forced me to justify every design choice, from font selection to the specific metaphors used to describe data decay. This reflection allowed me to see my own growth in audience adaptation and rhetorical rationale.

Regarding the minor assignments, the "What is Research?" in-class writing was eye-opening. I initially defined research as just "finding facts." By the end of that activity, I realized research is an iterative process of entering a conversation that is already happening, requiring a specific entrance strategy.

The "Source Evaluation" activity taught me how to vet white papers from NIST. As a Computer Science student, I tend to trust official documentation implicitly, but this activity taught me to look for the rhetorical bias and intended limitations even in high-level technical standards.

Our analysis of the "Pulp Fiction" diner scene was perhaps the most entertaining yet instructive minor assignment. It proved that rhetoric—specifically Ethos and Pathos—is present in every human interaction, from cinematic tension to corporate negotiations, and isn't limited to academic papers.

The "Genre Analysis" worksheet helped me deconstruct the "Open Letter" format. I learned that genres aren't just templates; they are social agreements. A letter to a CEO has a specific "set of rules" regarding brevity and professional urgency that, if broken, results in a loss of credibility.

During the "Reverse Outlining" session for Project 1, I saw the skeleton of my argument for the first time. This activity was crucial for my global revision, as it highlighted where my logic was circular or where I had jumped to conclusions without providing enough technical evidence for the reader.

The "Visual Rhetoric" workshop changed how I viewed my technical blog post. I learned that white space, bold headings, and infographics aren't just "decorations"—they are cognitive tools that guide the reader’s eye and emphasize key data points in a high-density information environment.

The "Citing in APA/MLA" quiz was more than just a test of punctuation. It taught me that citations are a form of Ethos. If my bibliography is messy or inconsistent, a professional reader will assume my data and code are also prone to errors, undermining my authority.

In the "Defining Rhetoric" discussion post, I explored the concept of Kairos. I realized that my PQC campaign was timely because of the recent NIST 2024 announcements. Writing at the "right time" for the right audience is just as important as the factual accuracy of the message itself.

The "Peer Review 1" session with Volodymyr was instrumental. He pointed out that my descriptions of "Q-Day" were too alarmist for an academic setting. His feedback helped me dial back the Pathos to make my Logos more credible to a community that values data over fear.

In "Peer Review 2," I learned the value of a "Generous Critic." Receiving feedback that was both harsh and helpful allowed me to strip away the "fluff" in my CEO letter and focus on the actuarial risk data that actually moves corporate policy.

The "Introductory Survey" at the start of the semester now feels like a relic. Looking back at my initial goals of simply "improving grammar," I can see how much I’ve grown from a "fact-reporter" to a "rhetorical strategist" who understands the social weight of technical communication.

The "Annotation Activity" on the A Writer’s Resource text helped me internalize the difference between local and global revision. Local revision is "fixing" errors, but global revision is "re-thinking" the entire approach to ensure the rhetorical goal is actually being met.

Our "Digital Studio" visit was a practical turning point for the e-Portfolio. Learning how to navigate the technical constraints of website hosting was a minor assignment that paved the way for the professional presentation of my work in this final project.

The "Ethos Workshop" taught me that my background in Computer Science gives me a specific kind of authority. I learned to leverage my "Technical Ethos" to make my warnings about decryption more persuasive by showcasing a deep understanding of the underlying algorithms.

The "Pathos in Advertising" activity showed me how to tap into the audience's values. For my project, I tapped into the CEO's value of "legacy" and "security" to make the PQC transition feel like a moral and strategic necessity rather than just a cost.

The "Drafting the Rationale" minor writing was the hardest. It required me to step out of the "creator" role and into the "critic" role, analyzing my own work as if I were a stranger to see if the intended message was actually the one being delivered.

The "Midterm Reflection" allowed me to recalibrate my trajectory. I realized I was spending too much time on the math and not enough on the "So What?" factor. This realization guided my revisions for Project 2 and helped me focus on audience impact.

The "MLA Style Manual" activity was a reminder of the "Constraints" of academic writing. Even as I try to be creative with my website design, I must respect the systemic rules of the discourse community I am writing for to maintain my professional standing.

The "Final Polish" activity for Project 2 was where I learned the power of the "Active Voice." Changing "The data was analyzed" to "We analyzed the data" made my campaign feel much more urgent, direct, and accountable to the stakeholders involved.

Reflecting on Professor Echavarria’s teaching, I found the "Recursive Logic" of the course to be the most effective. The professor didn't just teach us a concept and move on; we revisited Ethos, Pathos, and Logos in every single assignment, reinforcing their importance.

Professor Echavarria’s feedback was always "Actionable." In our 1-on-1 conference, he challenged me to make my CEO letter "sharper." He taught me that in professional writing, brevity is a form of respect for the reader's time and a marker of a confident writer.

The structure of the class—moving from research to multigenre to portfolio—felt like a professional pipeline. It mirrored the "Agile" development process I use in Computer Science, where we build, test, and iterate based on feedback from "users" or, in this case, readers.

Project 3 itself, this e-Portfolio, was the ultimate test of my "Visual Rhetoric" skills. Hosting on GitHub Pages allowed me to present my work in a medium that is native to my professional field, ensuring that my "Digital Ethos" is as strong as my written analysis.

In conclusion, this course has taught me that writing is not a soft skill—it is a technical system. Whether I am writing a line of code or an actuarial report, I am now equipped to analyze the "Genre," the "Audience," and the "Context" to ensure my message is not just received, but acted upon.
