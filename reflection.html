Course Reflection

Transitioning from technical writing to rhetorical writing involves not only penning down the thoughts but also designing a means to wield influence through language. The portfolio herein reflects my progress in negotiating the intricate social contract between the author and the reader, wherein the efficacy of communication is judged on its effect rather than just its precision. My ability to analyze the intricacies of a scientific controversy such as Post-Quantum Cryptography and distill sophisticated data for stakeholders such as college students to top business executives showcases my proficiency in rhetoric. Now, the transition to the rhetoric of technical communication has been a process of translating objective logic into strategic influence. Earlier, I thought about writing as an instrument for transmitting information and documentation. This course has completely changed my approach, making me realize that successful writing goes beyond information transfer and involves the skillful handling of readers' expectations.

Project 1 was the first time I experienced high-end investigative research, especially when concerned about the impending security threat of Post-Quantum Cryptography. While trying to combine mathematical principles with the story behind it, I had to ensure that it was comprehensible to any general academic audience. It was during this project that I discovered that no matter how data-driven our work may be, there is always a need for the correct context to create what we call "exigence," or the urgency for us to read the material. Through the proper presentation of technical expertise and the public domain, I realized that the creation of technical credibility was more than just having facts, and it was also about presenting the evidence strategically.

The Reflection from Project 1 involved self-analysis concerning the problem of the word count. At first, the target of 2,000 words seemed like a number chosen out of nowhere. In reality, however, a superfluous number of words were needed to provide the necessary logic to lattice-based mathematics.

The second project made me realize that the time has come to move from research to the stage of high stakes persuasion, which demanded a complex and elaborate multimodal campaign in three different artifacts. In the process of developing such an approach, I learned the true meaning of the 'social deal' of writing since I had to modify technical data into persuasive text that would appeal to completely different target audiences. I needed to make it persuasive and relevant to each person who will find my artifacts. It helped me to see the importance of tailoring my material according to the unique demands of the audience. Thus, every single artifact I prepared was not only useful but also effective in terms of convincing readers.

My Project 2 Reflection focused on the difficulty of switching voices. It was a meta-cognitive exercise that forced me to justify every design choice, from font selection to the specific metaphors used to describe data decay.

Regarding the minor assignments, "What is Research?" in-class writing was eye-opening. I initially defined research as just "finding facts." By the end of that activity, I realized research is an iterative process of entering a conversation that is already happening.

Our analysis of the "Pulp Fiction" diner scene was perhaps the most entertaining yet instructive minor assignment. It proved that rhetoric, specifically Ethos and Pathos, is present in every human interaction, not just in academic papers.

The Genre Analysis helped me deconstruct the Open Letter format. I learned that genres aren't just templates, and they are social agreements. A letter to a CEO has a specific "set of rules" that, if broken, results in a loss of credibility.

My initial impression of the Reverse Outlining project was that I discovered the skeleton of my argument. This particular activity played an important role during the global revision process. It allowed me to recognize logical flaws and unnecessary assumptions that I made based on my conclusions and personal views.

The Visual Rhetoric was extremely helpful when working on my technical blog post since it showed me that such elements as blank spaces, bold headings, and infographics should not be treated as mere decorations, and these elements are used to help readers navigate the content and point out the main data points.

The first Peer Review session was instrumental in refining my professional tone. Receiving a fresh perspective allowed me to see where my technical descriptions were becoming too alarmist for a professional setting. This general feedback helped me dial back the Pathos to make my Logos more credible to a community that values objective data over emotional appeals.

The Introductory Survey at the beginning of the term now seems very outdated to me. Reflecting on my objectives that I had set initially, I realize that I have come from being a “fact-reporter” to becoming a “rhetorical strategist.” I finally understood that my mistake was devoting too much attention to mathematics and not considering the "So What?" factor of the research.

The most difficult assignment among those listed is "Drafting the Rationale." Here, I had to put aside the identity of the creator and turn into the critic who assesses his own work from an outside perspective. Also, it is hard to explain what you mean exactly and stay on topic.

In the final polish related to Project 2, I realized the significance of the "Active Voice." For instance, changing "The data was analyzed" to "We analyzed the data" added an element of urgency and directness to my campaign.

Thinking back on the teaching methods of Professor Echavarria, I realized that the course methodology called “Recursive Logic” was the best way to learn. Professor Echavarria did not just give us some concepts and then move on, but all of them were mentioned and used in all our assignments.

The comments of Professor Echavarria could be characterized as "Actionable." He explained to me how I can make my writing stronger, precise, and persuasive. Also pointing out some key concepts to which I can refer and expand, develop in that matter.

The structure of the class was moving from research to multigenre to portfolio felt like a professional pipeline. It mirrored the Agile development process I use in Computer Science, where we build, test, and iterate.

Project 3 itself, this e-Portfolio, was the ultimate test of my "Visual Rhetoric" skills. Hosting on GitHub Pages allowed me to present my work in a medium that is native to my professional field, ensuring that my "Digital Ethos" is as strong as my writing. Also, this allows me to make it my own and unique.

In conclusion, this course has taught me that writing is a technical system. I am now equipped to analyze the "Genre," the "Audience," and the "Context" to ensure my message is not just received but acted upon; I can be heard and credible for people. I am very grateful to Professor Echavarria for this course and hope he liked it as much as I did.
